 A B S T R A C T
Breast cancer continues to be the primary cause of mortality from cancer in women globally, highlighting 
the urgent necessity for early and accurate identification. Although mammogram is the gold standard for 
screening, several challenges still exist, such as low contrast, varying sizes of masses, and irregular mass 
shapes. To address these limitations, we propose MANGA-YOLO, a novel learning model for breast mass 
detection inspired by the Mamba and transformer architecture. Our model integrates Mamba-Inspired Attention 
with Contextual Awareness (MACA) to improve feature extraction by capturing long-range dependencies and 
enhancing contextual understanding. Additionally, we introduce a Spatial and Channel Group Attention (SCGA) 
module, which combines convolutional spatial refinement with group self-attention. This enriches feature 
representation by strengthening boundary sensitivity and emphasizing global information through a series of 
attention modules, thus improving the detection of masses with diverse sizes. These improvements enable more 
precise detection of small, low-contrast, and irregularly shaped masses. The proposed approach is evaluated on 
three mammography datasets – INbreast, VinDr-Mammo, and CBIS-DDSM – achieving 0.881, 0.69, and 0.664 
for mAP50 metric, respectively. The results show the outperformance of our method over existing state-of-the-
art models. It suggests that MANGA-YOLO offers a more robust and efficient approach to automated breast 
mass detection, potentially improving early diagnosis and clinical decision making. The code is available at 
https://github.com/Kien-Trang/MANGA-YOLO.
1. Introduction
Breast cancer is still a major health concern worldwide, which is 
known as the most frequently diagnosed cancer and a leading cause of 
cancer-related deaths among women. It is anticipated that 3.2 million 
cases of breast cancer will be diagnosed, with annual deaths reaching 
1.1 million by 2050 [1]. However, the distribution of breast cancer in-
cidence and mortality are uneven across different regions. High-income 
countries report that survival rates have improved due to advances 
in screening and early detection in treatment. In contrast, low- and 
middle-income nations are still faced with late-stage diagnoses and lack 
of treatment access, which contributes to a high number of deaths [2]. 
Thus, early detection and accurate diagnosis are essential for effective 
treatment and improved survival rates. Furthermore, early identifica-
tion facilitates access to a wider range of treatment options, which can 
be less painful and targeted more precisely. This might substantially 
mitigate the physical and emotional effects of breast cancer treatment 
on patients.

Medical imaging continues to be an important cornerstone in the 
diagnosis and management of breast cancer. Various imaging modal-
ities play a pivotal role in the detection of breast masses, including 
Mammography, Ultrasound, Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), etc. 
However, in some cases, ultrasound and MRI are frequently employed 
as supplementary tools, especially in the evaluation of dense breast 
tissues and high-risk patient populations. Mammography remains the 
gold-standard tool for the detection of breast cancer [3], which facil-
itates the detection of tumors at an early stage. The use of screening 
mammography has demonstrated a decline in fatalities by up to 63%, 
as reported in [4]. Nevertheless, mammogram interpretation is often 
challenging due to factors such as breast density, low contrast between 
lesions and healthy tissue, etc.
In the last few years, emerging technologies and methodologies 
have enhanced breast cancer screening and diagnostic capabilities. The 
integration of artificial intelligence into radiological assessments has

shown promise in reducing diagnostic errors, especially in resource-

images through neural network training, demonstrating robust gener-

are introduced in object detection tasks with impressive performance. 
Nevertheless, these conventional CNN-based methods might not be 
efficient and optimal for medical object detection due to a lack of 
end-to-end training. Moreover, the Real-Time Detection Transformer 
(RT-DETR) [9] has recently emerged as an alternative in the domain of 
object detection. By using an advanced feature extraction backbone and 
a transformer-based design, RT-DETR demonstrates outstanding results 
across numerous tasks. On the other hand, the You Only Look Once 
(YOLO) model has emerged as a pivotal architecture, which is able to 
process the entire image through a single detection process. Due to the 
use of a single neural network model, the YOLO series models [10–12] 
have high inference speed and low computing cost, achieving real-time 
target detection. Another advantage is the ability to deal with objects 
of different scales and sizes with multi-scale feature fusion.
The architecture of YOLO typically consists of three parts: backbone, 
neck, and head. The backbone network is crucial for extracting valuable 
features from input images through the usage of CNNs, aiming to 
capture hierarchical features at different scales. Meanwhile, the neck 
part plays a key role in connecting the feature extraction stream from 
the backbone block to optimize information flow and enhance feature 
representation. At the end of the structure, the head part is treated 
as the block containing prediction layers. Through versions of YOLO, 
the models are more focused on improving the speed and accuracy 
of general detection. Starting from YOLOv4 [13], more complex ar-
chitectures like Spatial Pyramid Pooling (SPP) and Cross Stage Partial 
(CSP) are employed to improve the performance, while YOLOv5 [14] 
targets a lightweight network for faster speed with balanced accuracy. 
Besides, the reparameterization model is introduced from YOLOv6 [15] 
and coupled with the integration of an Efficient Layer Aggregation 
Network (ELAN) in YOLOv7 [16]. From YOLOv8 onwards [17–20], the 
decoupled structure is applied to separate classification and detection 
heads, improving the accuracy and efficiency of target detection. Al-
though recent advances in the YOLO series have significantly improved 
performance in general object detection tasks, there are still some 
limitations in working with medical image analysis. In particular, key 
challenges in analyzing mammograms include low contrast of certain 
medical structures, significant variability in mass size, and irregular 
mass shapes caused by complex surrounding tissue structures. These 
problems play an important role in the precise diagnosis of diseases.
Recently, Mamba has been introduced as a novel high-performance 
architecture [21], which has surpassed the transformer in some fields.
[22,23]. This model integrates a selective operation in the space models 
(SSM) with a hierarchical structure, allowing it to selectively process 
relevant information in an input-dependent manner. This enables the 
Mamba model to efficiently capture long-range dependencies. Thus, 
applying Mamba in vision tasks might boost the understanding ability 
of the model by working with long-distance spatial pixels.
In this paper, we propose an enhanced YOLO-based model with 
an application of breast mass detection. The proposed approach is 
designed based on the YOLOv11 architecture, which capitalizes on 
its architecture to extract more potential features from mammogram 
images. This enhancement specifically aims to augment the capacity 
of the original model for detecting the low-contrast and size vari-
ation mass regions. To achieve this, we have extended the original 
framework by integrating several innovative modules predominantly 
within the backbone and head part of the architecture. Notably, these 
modifications encompass the Mamba-inspired design with contextual 
awareness alongside the incorporation of enhancing group attention 
mechanism, thereby refining the detection capability of the model. This 
improvement facilitates an optimal integration of both local features 
and the surrounding context. These blocks improve the limitation of 
capturing the long dependencies with attention mechanisms, which 
might be neglected in the comparison of the traditional techniques.
The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
• We propose the Mamba-Inspired Attention with Contextual
Awareness (MACA) module to boost the ability to deal with 
additional latent features. This attention mechanism based on 
a Mamba-inspired structure is strengthened by an enhanced ca-
pacity for contextual understanding, which is integrated into 
different stages of the backbone for enhancing the performance 
of feature extraction. This contributes to the ability of detecting 
masses with low contrast and irregular shapes.
• We then construct a Spatial and Channel Group Attention (SCGA) 
module, which is placed before the head part. By integrating 
spatial and channel-based attention, the module not only utilizes 
a group-wise transformer mechanism but also enhances attention 
through convolutional operations, thus improving the detection 
performance of masses across different sizes.
• We introduce an improved architecture based on YOLO for breast 
mass detection. To the best of our knowledge, our approach 
is the first work, which introduces the Mamba-inspired design 
with group attention for addressing several challenges in breast 
mass detection. Specifically, the MACA module improves contex-
tual understanding to detect low contrast and irregularly shaped 
masses, while the SCGA module integrates group attention mech-
anisms to improve detection of masses with varying sizes by 
simultaneously refining spatial and channel-based attention. The 
evaluations are conducted on three distinct breast mass datasets: 
INbreast, CBIS-DDSM, and VinDr-Mammo. The results have note-
worthy enhancements in performance compared to previous stud-
ies and several recent detection models.
2. Related work
The advancement in deep learning has facilitated a significant 
amount of research on the implementation of automated breast cancer 
detection to assist in the diagnosis process. Early approaches mainly 
focused on Region of Interest (ROI)-based classification. These methods 
involved a preliminary cropping step to extract the ROI, followed 
by classifying the extracted regions as either normal or containing 
masses. The well-known pre-trained models that are usually used in 
classification are ResNet [24], GoogleNet [25], AlexNet [26], etc. On 
the other hand, the later studies focused more on end-to-end training 
such as Faster Region-based Convolutional Neural Network (Faster-
RCNN) [27], YOLO series [10]–[20], Single Shot MultiBox Detector 
(SSD) [28]. These methods eliminate the need for pre-processing input 
images into smaller patches, thereby streamlining the process and 
improving efficiency.
2.1. Region of interest-based classification
A self-design deep convolutional neural network is applied in the 
work [29] to classify the ROIs for breast mass. The framework in-
corporates data pre-processing, augmentation, and transfer learning 
techniques to enhance performance on the MIAS and private datasets. 
The results demonstrate a test accuracy of 97% in distinguishing be-
nign and malignant masses. In order to improve the performance, 
the study [30] utilized the combined framework from different pre-
trained models such as AlexNet and GoogleNet to classify the small 
patches of the image. Thanks to the deep fusion features, the metrics 
are improved when compared to the single model. K. B. Soulami 
et al. [31] introduced the capsule network (CapsNet) to deal with the 
patch-based breast mass classification. This newly proposed architec-
ture deals with computational time issues, which usually occur in high 
hierarchical structures. The model gives an AUC of 0.997 for binary 
classification and 0.9 for multi-class scenarios, which outperforms the

2.2. End-to-end detection
A two-stage deep learning method based on Faster R-CNN and a 
dedicated tumor classification model is proposed by [33] for work-
ing with high-resolution mammograms. This method significantly en-
hanced the Mean Average Precision at 50% Intersection over Union 
(mAP50) score from 0.85 to 0.94 on their self-collected dataset, which 
surpasses the Faster R-CNN and SSD. ERetinaNet [34] presents a spe-
cialized detection framework tailored for mammographic mass detec-
tion, which is built based on the RetinaNet architecture [35]. The 
model introduces an Efficient Channel Attention (ECA) mechanism and 
a modified feature pyramid network to increase the attention ability for 
target region while maintaining computational efficiency. Besides, Vi-
sion Transformer (ViT) is also integrated to enhance the global semantic 
information. A. Baccouche et al. [36] focus on an end-to-end YOLOv3-
based system for simultaneous detection and classification of breast 
lesions. By employing a fusion model strategy, they achieved a bal-
ance between precision and detection accuracy, significantly improving 
performance across datasets. Their results demonstrated detection per-
formance reaching 0.817 and 0.737 of mAP50 scores for INbreast and 
CBIS-DDM datasets, respectively. Another later version is also explored 
in the work [37] by utilizing YOLOv5x and YOLOv5s models for de-
tecting breast masses in mammograms. The authors employed transfer 
learning to improve model generalization and evaluated mainly their 
approach on the VinDr-Mammo dataset, and the MIAS dataset is used 
for external testing. The best results achieved with YOLOv5x is 0.602 
mAP50 for the VinDr-Mammo dataset, while only 0.593 mAP50 is 
reached by YOLOv5s. Similarly, F. Prinzi et al. [38] also explored 
multiple YOLO architectures on their joint self-proprietary dataset. 
The YOLOv3, YOLOv5, and YOLOv5-Transformer are integrated with 
Eigen-CAM, which aims to reduce false negatives and provide out-
put explanation. The evaluation shows the effectiveness of the YOLO 
architecture in breast mass detection with 0.621 for mAP50, 0.726 
for precision, and 0.591 for recall. The study [39] proposed a hy-
brid YOLO and transformer-based model for simultaneous detection 
and segmentation of breast masses in mammograms. This novel ap-
proach combined YOLOv5L6 for high-resolution mass localization with 
a Local–Global (LOGO) architecture to enhance capturing long-range 
dependencies, which dramatically improves the performance at the 
pixel level. Evaluated on CBIS-DDSM and INbreast datasets, YOLO-
LOGO achieved a result of 0.65 and 0.6 for mAP50, respectively. 
E. Mahoro et al. [40] introduced the use of YOLOv7 and YOLOv8 
for detecting breast masses in mammograms. Their framework in-
tegrates pre-processing techniques, such as contrast-limited adaptive 
histogram equalization (CLAHE) and median filtering, to enhance im-
age quality and reduce noise. The study utilizes the VinDr-Mammo 
dataset and reports superior performance with YOLOv8, achieving a 
mAP50 score of 0.65. N. F. Razali et al. [41] propose a novel en-
hancement YOLO-based method to address the challenges of breast 
mass detection in mammograms, particularly in dense breast tissues. 
They introduced Spatial-based Breast Density Enhancement for Mass 
Detection (SbBDEM), which optimizes the image pre-processing stage 
by adjusting contrast and enhancing textural features to improve the 
visibility of masses. By employing a modified YOLOv3 architecture, the 
study achieves significant improvements, achieving 0.8125 for mAP50 
score. Nevertheless, the existing studies tried to focus on increasing the 
image quality to improve the performance of detecting breast mass. 
They rely on general pre-processing methods rather than incorporating 
specialized mechanisms or improving the internal representation learn-
ing of models. These pre-processing methods are applied uniformly 
across all images without leveraging semantic knowledge, and this may 
lead to inadvertently enhancing noise or suppressing the details of 
pathological patterns. Thus, our aim is to design explicitly modules 
integrated inside the architecture for overcoming the drawbacks of the 
previous studies in the case of breast mass detection.
3. Materials and methods
3.1. Datasets
This study is mainly conducted on three distinct datasets to de-
tect the breast mass cancer region based on mammograms. These 
datasets provide diverse imaging views, annotation practices, and types 
of breast density, which enables a comprehensive evaluation of the 
proposed framework.
The INbreast dataset [42] is one of the well-annotated and long-
lasting datasets designed for research in breast cancer diagnosis and 
mammographic imaging. It provides full-field digital mammograms 
(FFDMs), which are collected in a clinical setting and annotated by 
expert radiologists. The dataset includes 115 cases, comprising a total 
of 410 mammogram images in the resolution of 3328 × 4084 or 
2560 × 3328 pixels.
The VinDr-Mammo [43] is known as one of the recent massive 
datasets of breast cancer mammograms provided by the Institutional 
Review Board of Hanoi Medical University Hospital and Hospital 108. 
This dataset was ultimately collected in Vietnam, which also offers a 
valuable reference specifically for Southeast Asian women. It consists 
of approximately 20,000 images in the resolution of 2800 × 3518 pixels 
covering various breast density levels.
The Curated Breast Imaging Subset of Digital Database for Screening 
Mammography (CBIS-DDSM) is proposed by R. S. Lee et al. [44]. The 
dataset is built upon the original DDSM dataset, which is refined with 
higher-quality scans and more precise annotations. It includes over 
2500 mammographic cases with a mean of 3138 × 5220 pixels, each 
Consisting of Craniocaudal (CC) and Mediolateral Oblique (MLO) views 
with corresponding pathology diagnoses.
3.2. Data pre-processing
Since the Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine (DI-
COM) image type contains rich in metadata and high bit-depth pixel 
information, which is often incompatible with deep learning frame-
works. To allow the object detection model to directly interpret the 
images, we converted the DICOM format into JPG format. This con-
version also reduces the computational overhead and memory usage. 
In order to fit into the model, the input images are then resized to 
640 × 640 pixel with the 8 bit-depth per channel. For INbreast dataset, 
107 malignant images with annotated mass ROIs are considered for 
use in this study. Similarly, not all the images in VinDr-Mammo are 
fully annotated in the mass region, only 1029 images with the full 
label are employed for conducting experiments. Concentrating on mass 
analysis, 1514 images from the CBIS-DDSM dataset are selected with 
mass annotations.

3.3. MANGA-YOLO architecture
The primary framework of the proposed work is fundamentally 
based on YOLOv11. Consequently, certain fundamental blocks are re-
tained to ensure the benefits of YOLO-based architecture. The overview 
of MANGA-YOLO model is depicted in Fig.  1, which illustrates the 
components and processing pipeline for breast mass detection. Initially, 
the model receives a 640 × 640 mammogram image as input and pro-
cesses it through a series of convolutional layers and Mamba-Inspired 
Attention with Contextual Awareness (MACA) modules, which enhance 
feature extraction by capturing long-range dependencies and contextual 
information. The ending part of the backbone adopts Spatial Pyramid 
Pooling Fast (SPPF) and Cross-Stage Partial Spatial Attention (C2PSA) 
modules to refine multi-scale features and improve spatial attention. 
The extracted features are then passed through the upsampling and con-
catenation operations to retain fine-grained details. Spatial and Channel 
Group Attention (SCGA) modules are then integrated to enhance object 
detection capabilities. The detection head consists of depth-wise and 
standard convolutional layers, optimizing the bounding box regression 
and classification loss functions for accurate mass localization.
The primary framework of the proposed work is fundamentally 
based on YOLOv11. Consequently, certain fundamental blocks includ-
ing SPFF, C2PSA and Detect are retained to ensure the benefits of 
YOLO-based architecture, which is shown in Fig.  2. SPFF module is 
applied near the end of backbone, which aims to deal with various 
sizes of objects. This is specifically designed to extract image features 
at multiple scales, which enhances the capacity of models to recognize 
objects across diverse spatial dimensions. The operation is achieved 
through a series of max-pooling operations applied with varying kernel 
sizes, which enables the module to capture and combine multi-scale 
contextual information effectively. Furthermore, the Cross-Stage Partial 
Spatial Attention (C2PSA) module is integrated as an ending block 
of the backbone to enhance the attention mechanism. This module 
is designed to emphasize spatial relationships within feature maps, 
which enables the model to focus on the most critical regions of an 
image. The internal structure consists of dual Partial Spatial Attention 
(PSA) modules, each operating on separate branches to extract comple-
mentary spatial features. This architectural design enables the model 
to effectively prioritize spatial information while ensuring an optimal 
trade-off between processing speed and accuracy. Finally, the Detection 
part employs a decoupled head, which separates the bounding box

In the second path of the MACA block, we implemented contex-

potential features for the Mamba-inspired attention. Initially, the local 
features and surrounding context features are extracted via the standard

where MACA is the final result after aggregation, the ⊕ operation

3.6. Bounding box optimization
In the YOLO-based model, optimizing the loss function and applying 
Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) are essential for achieving robust 
and accurate object detection. This process is used to ensure that the 
model predicts bounding box coordinates and classifies detected objects 
accurately. However, despite these optimizations, models often produce 
several overlapping bounding boxes for the same object due to the 
nature of anchor boxes and grid-based detection. NMS is mentioned as 
a post-processing step to eliminate redundant detections by selecting 
the bounding box with the highest confidence score and suppressing 
others with a high Intersection over Union (IoU).
Although this NMS-based implementation is effective in improving 
the final result, standard IoU does not account for the spatial relation-
ship between object boxes and the anchor boxes. Thus, DIoU [47] is 
proposed to overcome the drawbacks while keeping the advantages 
of the original IoU. Besides considering the distance between boxes, 
DIoU can effectively handle cases where two bounding boxes overlap. 
However, it remains limited in scenarios where the center points of the 
two boxes overlap, which brings the limitation in optimization. Thus, 
the aspect ratio of the box is considered as a promised direction to over-
come this problem. Several IoU variants such as SIoU [48], EIoU [49], 
and CIoU [50] have been proposed to further improve bounding box 
regression performance. SIoU introduces an incorporation of angle cost 
and scale adaptation to enhance multiple objects detection with varying 
orientations and scales. This might increase the computational expense 
during training and does not always provide a substantial performance 
gain due to adding more terms in loss function. In addition, EIoU also 
proposed by processing width and height differences separately, which 
aims to improve bounding box regression. However, by individually 
penalizing width and height might struggle to provide the same level 
of precision, making it less effective for small object detection tasks. 
While SIoU and EIoU offer improvements in the optimization process, 
their applicability might exceed the needs in scenarios involving mass 
detection within a single class, especially when detecting objects of very 
small size.
On the other hand, CIoU reduces localization errors, particularly for 
extended or irregularly shaped objects, by considering the normalized 
distance between the anticipated and ground truth bounding boxes, and 
therefore, preserving shape consistency. The loss function of CIoU is

4. Experiments
4.1. Implementation details
Regarding the evaluation, all experiments are run on the computer 
with the CPU Intel core i7-12700 and an RTX 3090 GPU including 
24Gb memory. The operating system is Ubuntu 20.04 LTS, while the 
development framework is Pytorch 2.0 and CUDA 11.7. In this study, 
we employed a random image-level partitioning strategy, where 80% 
of the images were used for training, and 20% for testing. Importantly, 
our model is specifically designed as a single-image-based detection 
framework, which does not rely on multi-view fusion or contextual 
information from paired craniocaudal (CC) and mediolateral oblique 
(MLO) views from the same patient. Therefore, each image is treated 
as an independent sample during both training and inference. The 
resolution of the images in the datasets is resized to 640 × 640, while 
the batch size is set to 32 with the training processed in 300 epochs. 
The training optimizer is conducted by AdamW, starting with a learning 
rate of 0.01.

The performance comparison presented in Table  1 highlights the 
effectiveness of the proposed MANGA-YOLO model against the latest 
object detection architectures, including YOLOv9, YOLOv10, YOLOv11, 
and RT-DETR, across three benchmark datasets: INbreast,
VinDr-Mammo, and CBIS-DDSM. Our model achieves the highest recall, 
mAP50 and mAP50-95, for the INbreast and VinDr-Mamo datasets. 
For the CBIS-DDSM dataset, our model still maintains its robustness, 
outperforming competing models in mAP50 for 0.664 and mAP50-
95 for 0.274, highlighting its adaptability across different datasets. 
In addition, we conducted cross-dataset experiments, where models 
were trained on one dataset and tested on the remaining two, as 
shown in Table  2. Across these settings, MANGA-YOLO still achieves 
higher precision and mAP than others, with particularly strong results 
when trained on CBIS-DDSM and tested on INbreast and VinDr-Mammo 
dataset — reaching mAP50 of 0.625 and mAP50-95 of 0.263. These 
results demonstrate that the proposed modules generalize beyond 
dataset-specific biases. Table  3 shows the performance comparison of 
the proposed model and the previous studies. For the INbreast dataset, 
MANGA-YOLO achieves 0.881 for mAP50 and 0.566 for mAP50-95, 
surpassing the previous best - 0.8125 for mAP50. Similarly, for VinDr-
Mammo, our model attains the mAP50 of 0.69, outperforming the 
approach of E. Mahoro et al. [40]. In CBIS-DDSM, the proposed model 
remains competitive with a mAP50 of 0.664.
To evaluate the feasibility of our model, a comparative analy-
sis of inference time, model parameters, and FLOPs is performed, as 
described in Table  4. Although MANGA-YOLO introduces two addi-
tional modules, the overall architectural design remains lightweight, 
with only 2.94 million parameters and 8.3 GFLOPs. These values 
are only slightly higher than YOLOv10 and YOLOv11. Notably, our 
model achieves an inference time of 1.2 ms per image, which is well 
within the threshold for real-time deployment. The results confirm 
that MANGA-YOLO balances accuracy with practical speed, making 
it highly suitable for integration into time-sensitive practical applica-
tions. Furthermore, we conducted a Free-Response Receiver Operating

achieve lower values. These results demonstrate that MANGA-YOLO 
achieves not only high performance in detection but also offers a more 
suitable trade-off between sensitivity and false positives.

line YOLOv11 model. Besides, the performance continues to improve 
with the integration of SCGA, which is incorporated before different 
detection blocks to effectively handle objects of varying sizes. Overall, 
the combination of MACA and SCGA at all three mentioned locations 
gives the most balanced performance across all metrics. Therefore, this 
implementation has been chosen as the primary architecture for the 
proposed model.
4.5.2. Effect of feature extraction backbone
The performance comparison of other feature extraction blocks 
and the proposed MACA is demonstrated in Table  6. Attentional Fea-
ture Fusion (AFF) [51], Multi-scale Attention Network (MAN) [52], 
RepVGG [53] are employed to replace the feature extraction blocks 
in the main architecture. Indeed, the MACA consistently outperforms 
other feature extractors, which achieves the best balance between 
all key metrics. It gives the highest recall and mAP50 on the IN-
breast dataset, and continues to reach top performance in three out of 
four metrics on both the VinDr-Mamo and CBIS-DDSM datasets. This 
superior performance is attributed to the joint effect of two subcom-
ponents within MACA: the Mamba-inspired Attention (MA) path and 
the Contextual Awareness (CA) path. The MA path aims to improve 
long-range feature modeling, while the CA path enhances fine-grained 
spatial localization. Thus, these components contribute complemen-
tary strengths that enhance the effectiveness of MACA in capturing 
fine-grained details in low contrast regions and reducing the risk of 
overlooking detections.
4.5.3. Effect of attention mechanism
In comparison to Triplet Attention (TA) [54], Squeeze-and-
Excitation Block (SE) [55], Coordinate Attention (CA) [56], the pro-
posed SCGA demonstrates better results in almost all metrics, as il-
lustrated in Table  7. SCGA surpasses current attention mechanisms in 
mAP50 and mAP50-95, indicating improved mass localization. This 
enhancement is attributed to its refined attention mechanism, which 
allows for better detection of objects with varying sizes and shapes. As 
a result, our proposed model is able to detect even small-sized masses, 
contributing to a direct improvement in the overall detection result.
4.5.4. Effect of bounding box optimization
The primary objective of this optimization is to improve the bound-
ing box regression for object detection tasks. Various versions of IoU 
have been proposed as improvements over the traditional method. In 
this work, DIoU [47], SIoU [48], EIoU [49] and CIoU [50] are evaluated 
to determine the most effective method for enhancing localization 
accuracy, as presented in Table  8. This suggests that CIoU reached the 
optimal result with almost reaching the highest metrics compared to 
the others. Thus, CIoU is confirmed as the optimization selection for 
integrating into our proposed model, which ensures better detection 
accuracy and robustness.
5. Conclusion
We developed MANGA-YOLO, a novel YOLO-based architecture 
to improve breast mass detection in mammograms. By incorporating 
MACA and SCGA modules, the model refines feature extraction and 
strengthens attention mechanisms, leading to better detection per-
formance. The results of INbreast, VinDr-Mammo, and CBIS-DDSM 
datasets demonstrate superior performance in various metrics com-
pared to state-of-the-art models. The integration of MACA enables 
the model to capture long-range dependencies and contextual under-
standing, while SCGA enhances multiple attention in different aspects 
to improve object detection with various sizes. Moreover, the use of 
CIoU for bounding box optimization further improves the training 
performance, ensuring robustness across diverse cases. Ablation studies 
confirm that each proposed module significantly contributes in im-
proving performance, which highlights the effectiveness of the overall
